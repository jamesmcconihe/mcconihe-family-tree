The MacConnochie–McConihe descent

One family, one name, four spellings, three countries. This chart lays out the working hypothesis for the ancestry of John McConihe (born 22 December 1669, Argyllshire; emigrated to Londonderry, New Hampshire, 1718), tracing the family tradition of descent from the MacConnochie Campbells of Inverawe. Every box is color-coded by the strength of its evidence, and every person links to the index below. The central open question of the whole research: who was John's father?

Status: links shown in amber or red are not established to genealogical proof standard. They mark where the evidence currently points and where research is active — not settled conclusions. Documented boxes rest on the independent, converging work of Diarmid Campbell and Gordon MacGregor plus primary records.
Revised 13 July 2026
The printed Privy Council entry of 30 November 1613 (RPC, 1st ser., X, pp. 177–179) has now been consulted directly (HathiTrust page images of the University of California copy; NRS verification pending). It explicitly names Archibald and Dougall as sons of the Tutor — confirming Gordon MacGregor's reading — and lists Lachlan and Duncan McDonachie Campbell immediately beside them, unfiliated: possibly two further sons, and the likely source of the alternative name-trio recalled by Diarmid Campbell. It also shows Patrick, the Tutor's brother, outlawed with his nephews, and the Earl of Argyll disclaiming the whole group on oath. The full Red Book of Scotland Vol. 3 entry for Campbell of Inverawe (pp. 781–790) has been read in full, yielding record citations RD1/167 (Barbara Graham's 1607 bond), RD1/277 (Patrick "MacDonochie alias Campbell," 1617), RPC V:241 (the Tutor, 1595) and RPC VI:442 (his brother Duncan, 1602).

The 30 November 1613 record — now consulted

The complaint by Alexander Colquhoun of Luss and others against the Clan Gregor and their assisters, heard at Edinburgh on 30 November 1613, has been read in the printed Register of the Privy Council, First Series, Vol. X, pp. 177–179 (record: Decreta June 1612–Dec. 1613, Fol. 278a–279b). The pursuers are the Colquhoun kindred and some five hundred herried tenants of Luss; the defenders are rebels unrelaxed from hornings of 27 & 29 May and 1 & 3 June 1613. Among them, verbatim:

"…Archibald McDonachie Campbell, son of the Tutor of Innerraw, Dougall McCondochie Campbell, brother of the said Archibald, Lauchlane McDonachie Campbell, Duncane, his brother, servitor to the said Archibald, Patrik McDonachie Campbell 'father bruther' to the Laird of Innerraw…"

The pursuers argued the Earl of Argyll was answerable for the defenders as his tenants; the Earl, personally present, swore they were not, and the Lords assoilzied him. None of the four McDonachie Campbell kinsmen is traced in the record thereafter. Implication for the main question: the candidate pool of emigrant ancestors now potentially numbers four rebel kinsmen of the Tutor's generation and household — outlawed, disclaimed by their chief, and invisible to Scottish records after 1613 — any of whom could be the grandfather of John, b. 1669.

Index of persons

Duncan Campbell · progenitor, late 1300s documented
Eponym of the kindred — his descendants are Mac Dhonnchaidh, "sons of Duncan." Both modern scholars converge on Duncan, son of Gillespic (Archibald) Campbell of Lochawe; a Great Seal charter of 15 March 1369 names an ancestral Duncan in the barony of Lochawe. [DC · RBS3]
Archibald Campbell of Inverawe · fl. c. 1570 documented
Laird of Inverawe; father of Dougall, John dubh, Patrick, and Duncan. [GM · DC]
Dougall Campbell of Inverawe · d. c. 1583 documented
Laird; died leaving an infant heir, Archibald ("the pupil"), which brought his brother John dubh to power as Tutor. [DC]
John dubh MacConnochie Campbell, Tutor of Inverawe · c. 1557 – bef. 15 July 1607 documented
Guardian of Inverawe for ~20 years; ruled the kindred without owning it — hence no sasine trail. Subject of a Privy Council complaint, 2 December 1595, for having "spuilyied the whole goods and plenishing" at Ardowaneck in Breadalbane [RPC V:241]. In 1596 English intelligence reported him ready to lead 1,000 men to Ireland to aid Tyrone — he had "friends in Ireland"; the Clan Campbell Society (North America) further states he led the Scots gallowglasses fighting in Ireland at the end of the 16th century (basis being sought from Diarmid Campbell — if verified, a direct family link to Ireland two generations before the 1678 migration). Said to have been killed by his nephew on the pupil's coming of age; the record shows only that he had died by 15 July 1607, when his widow granted her bond [RD1/167]. [DC · GM · RBS3 · RPC · CCSNA]
Patrick & Duncan Campbell · the Tutor's brothers documented
Patrick, tacksman of DubhChoille: named among the unrelaxed rebels of 30 Nov 1613 as "Patrik McDonachie Campbell, 'father bruther' [paternal uncle] to the Laird of Innerraw," placed at Acharne with his own servitors [RPC X:179]; four years later, as "Patrick MacDonochie alias Campbell," styled uncle to Archibald of Inverawe in his renunciation of the two merk lands of Auchnacraig to Hugh MacCorquodale of Phantilands, 6 Nov 1617 [RD1/277] — a family member appearing under the patronymic in a legal record. Duncan: denounced rebel by the Privy Council, 6 August 1602, for the theft of five cows with calves from Andrew MacBaith in Hilton of Maler (Nov 1598) [RPC VI:442]. [RPC · RBS3 · DC · GM]
The lairds of Inverawe · 1607–1705, all traced documented
Archibald the pupil (d. bef. 1648, six younger sons); Lt-Col Dougall (second-in-command of Argyll's 1642 regiment for Ireland, d. 1666); Archibald, Governor of Duart, d.s.p. 21 May 1705. Estate passed via his brother Duncan of Crunachy's son; sold 1765. Every main-line male in the 1640s–80s is accounted for in Scotland. Note: the 1722 entail of the later Archibald of Inverawe names his brothers Dugald, Lachlan and Alexander — a recurring Dugald/Lachlan name-cluster in the family, and the likely source of the alternative trio recalled in correspondence for the 1613 entry. [DC · RBS3]
John Campbell, "Iain og" · son of the Tutor documented · untracked
Only legitimate son, by Barbara Graham. Named with his mother in her bond of 15 July 1607 to George Schaw of Knockhill for 200 merks Scots [RD1/167] as she departed for the Lowlands. Married the widow of William M'Clellan of Mullock. No descendants traced — the root of the Meadowbank conjecture. [DC 84–85 · GM corr. · RBS3]
Archibald Campbell · son of the Tutor, fl. 1613 documented · untracked
Explicitly styled "son of the Tutor of Innerraw" in the Privy Council complaint of 30 Nov 1613 (Colquhoun of Luss v. Clan Gregor and assisters), now consulted in the printed register [RPC X:179]. Unrelaxed from hornings of May–June 1613; the Earl of Argyll disclaimed him on oath. Untracked thereafter. A leading candidate grandfather of the immigrant line. [RPC X:177–179 · GM corr. · RBS3]
Dougall Campbell · son of the Tutor, fl. 1613 documented · untracked
"Dougall McCondochie Campbell, brother of the said Archibald" — the second explicitly filiated rebel son [RPC X:179]. Same outlawry, same disappearance from the record. Candidate grandfather of the immigrant line. [RPC X:177–179 · GM corr. · RBS3]
Lachlan & Duncan McDonachie Campbell · fl. 1613 documented · filiation unproven
Named immediately after the Tutor's sons in the 1613 defender list: "Lauchlane McDonachie Campbell, Duncane, his brother, servitor to the said Archibald" [RPC X:179]. The record ties them only to each other and to Archibald's service — it does not call them sons of the Tutor. They may be two further, unfiliated sons (relevant to the Tutor's visit to Finlarig in June 1597 with three sons) or close kin in his household. Either way: outlawed 1613, untracked after, and within the candidate pool for the emigrant ancestor. [RPC X:179]
Maconochies of Meadowbank · Midlothian conjecture
Edinburgh legal family, documented from the early 1700s; acquired the Meadowbank estate mid-1700s. Their own tradition claims Argyll descent; no pre-1700 Argyll property record found (consistent with a landless origin). A pedigree sits in Argyll Papers bundle 368 at Inveraray. [GM corr. · ARG-11-02-109]
Allan Maconochie, Lord Meadowbank · 1748–1816 tradition
Court of Session judge from 1796; the title later held by his son Alexander — the "chief" named in the 1867 McConihe memoir. Under the hypothesis, cousins of the American line, not ancestors. [IM · NRS NG3/4/31]
Father of John — unknown · b. ~1630s–40s unknown
The open research question and the goal of the whole project. Leading candidate pool, sharpened by the 1613 record: a son of one of the four outlawed McDonachie Campbell kinsmen — Archibald or Dougall (sons of the Tutor), or Lachlan or Duncan (possible sons) — all unrelaxed rebels disclaimed by Argyll on oath and invisible to Scottish records after 30 Nov 1613. Secondary pool: untracked younger sons of the laird line (six sons of the pupil, d. bef. 1648). All documented main-line males of the window are accounted for in Scotland. Paths to him: Argyll parish registers; bundle 368 pedigrees; Ulster records under McConaghy-type forms; Y-DNA. [analysis of RPC X · DC · RBS3 · GM corr.]
John McConihe · b. 22 Dec 1669, Argyllshire tradition → documented 1718
The immigrant ancestor. Birth date and Argyllshire origin from family tradition; his father is the open research question. [IM · BC · DB]
Londonderry, Ireland · 1678, tradition tradition
Family tradition places the household's relocation to Derry in 1678 and John in the 1689 siege. Not in the 1867 memoir — carried by the Bureau County (1885) and DuBin streams. Ulster name form: McConaghy. [BC · DB]
Londonderry, New Hampshire · 1718, documented documented
John arrived with the Ulster-Scots settlers of Nutfield, 1718; home lot recorded 25 Feb 1722/3 as "McConoghy." [ER II:80]
New Hampshire descendants · to Isaac McConihe, 1787–1867 documented
John of Merrimack, then Isaac McConihe of Troy, N.Y. (b. 22 Aug 1787), whose 1867 memorial volume preserves the family tradition — including the names Inverawe, Maconochy, and Meadowbank. [IM · ER]
